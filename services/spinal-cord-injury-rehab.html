Understanding Spinal Cord Injury

A spinal cord injury (SCI) occurs when trauma, disease or degeneration damages the spinal cord — the bundle of nerves that relays signals between the brain and the rest of the body. The consequences depend critically on the level of the injury (how high up the spine) and whether the injury is complete (total loss of function below the injury level) or incomplete (some signals still pass through, preserving partial movement or sensation). Cervical (neck) injuries typically affect all four limbs and trunk. Thoracic injuries affect the trunk and lower limbs. Lumbar and sacral injuries affect the lower limbs, bladder and bowel.

Beyond motor and sensory deficits, SCI can affect breathing (in high cervical injuries), autonomic function (blood pressure, temperature regulation, bladder, bowel), and sexual function. The life impact is profound, and without expert, sustained rehabilitation, secondary complications including pressure sores, joint contractures, respiratory infections, muscle wasting and deep vein thrombosis can further reduce quality of life and longevity.

The Role of Physiotherapy in SCI Rehabilitation

Physiotherapy is the cornerstone of SCI rehabilitation at every stage — from the acute phase in hospital to long-term home-based management. In the acute phase, the physiotherapist focuses on respiratory care, positioning to prevent pressure injuries, passive range of motion to prevent contractures, and beginning gentle active exercise of any muscles with preserved function. As the patient stabilises, therapy progressively shifts to strengthening, transfer training, mobility and independence skills.

For incomplete SCI — where some voluntary movement remains — the neuroplasticity principles that underpin stroke rehabilitation apply equally. Consistent, repetitive movement training can strengthen neural pathways and partially restore function over months to years of dedicated therapy. Techniques including body-weight-supported treadmill training, functional electrical stimulation (FES), robotic-assisted gait training, and intensive task-specific practice all have evidence supporting their use in SCI rehabilitation.

Even for complete SCI, where restoration of motor function below the injury level is not achievable, physiotherapy remains critically important. Maximising strength in preserved muscles above the injury level, training safe and efficient wheelchair use and transfers, managing spasticity and pain, protecting skin integrity and maintaining cardiovascular health through adapted exercise are all essential physiotherapy goals that directly impact longevity, health and quality of life.

Our SCI Rehabilitation Process

A comprehensive five-stage programme addressing all aspects of spinal cord injury rehabilitation

1

Neurological Assessment & Level Determination

Detailed ASIA impairment scale assessment, motor and sensory level testing, functional independence evaluation, and respiratory and cardiovascular screening.

2

Respiratory & Cardiovascular Optimisation

Breathing exercises, postural drainage, supported coughing techniques, and adapted cardiovascular training to prevent respiratory complications and maintain heart health.

3

Strength & Mobility Training

Progressive strengthening of preserved muscle groups, passive range of motion for paralysed limbs, spasticity management, and mobility training appropriate to injury level.

4

Transfer & Wheelchair Skills Training

Safe transfer techniques between bed, wheelchair and toilet, wheelchair propulsion and manoeuvring skills, pressure relief techniques, and pressure sore prevention education.

5

Home Environment Adaptation Guidance

Assessment and recommendations for home modifications, adaptive equipment, carer training in safe handling, and a long-term self-management programme for patient and family.

Benefits of SCI Rehabilitation Physiotherapy

What dedicated spinal cord injury physiotherapy achieves

Improved Strength

Maximising strength in preserved muscle groups above and at the injury level enables better transfers, functional independence and mobility.

Better Transfers

Safe, efficient transfer techniques between bed, wheelchair and other surfaces are essential for daily living and reducing dependence on carers.

Respiratory Function

Breathing exercises and respiratory physiotherapy significantly reduce the risk of pneumonia and chest infections — a leading cause of SCI-related hospitalisation.

Pressure Sore Prevention

Education in pressure relief techniques, optimal positioning and skin inspection reduces the risk of pressure ulcers — a serious and preventable SCI complication.

Independence Skills

Training in activities of daily living — personal care, cooking, community access — using adaptive techniques and equipment to maximise self-sufficiency.

Quality of Life

Comprehensive SCI rehabilitation improves not just physical function but also mental wellbeing, social participation and overall life satisfaction.
